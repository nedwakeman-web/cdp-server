THE SCIENCE
What spiritual practice has always known,
neuroscience is now proving.

Your brain processes 11 million bits of information every second. Your conscious mind handles around 50. What makes it through that filter is determined by what you hold as important, and that is trainable.

When you begin your day with a clear orienting frame, your brain's salience network calibrates accordingly, noticing the relevant and suppressing the noise. The cosmic framework is the instruction to the filter.

This is the neuroscience of selective attention, established at Oxford in 1958 (Broadbent), confirmed in fMRI studies of the default mode and salience networks (Technical University of Munich, 2022) and synthesised by Stanford neurosurgeon James Doty in the most current statement of the field (Mind Magic, 2024).

No belief required. Just the architecture of your own mind.

THE NEUROSCIENCE

Your brain processes roughly 11 million bits of sensory data every second. Conscious awareness handles around 50. The gap is bridged by the salience network (the brain's relevance filter, centred in the anterior insula and anterior cingulate cortex), which decides what surfaces to awareness based on what you have already marked as important. Set a frame in the morning and hold it clearly, and the brain's filter shifts to surface what aligns with it throughout the day. This is cognitive priming: pre-activating a mental schema increases the speed and probability of recognising related experience. It does not require belief. It requires attention.

THE NEUROSCIENCE

The human brain does not perform uniformly across the day. Circadian rhythms (the 24-hour biological cycles governed by the suprachiasmatic nucleus, your brain's master clock) produce measurable fluctuations in attention, working memory, executive function and self-belief formation (how readily you update your beliefs about yourself and your capacities). A 2024 preprint found suppressed self-belief updating during the early afternoon. A Cambridge study (Mehrhof and Nord, eLife, 2025, n=994) confirmed that effort-based decision-making is directly modulated by circadian phase.
